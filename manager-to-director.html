Part 3 — The Ceiling You're Hitting

Here's what's happening inside your head right now, if you're the person I'm writing this for:

You've been the manager for 3+ years. You run a team. Your SOC hums. Your audits pass. Your board reports go in on time. Technically, you are the strongest person in the room on most days.

And yet.

The Director role opens up. Maybe at your company, maybe at another. You apply. You interview. You don't get it. The person who got it was less technical than you. Sometimes significantly less.

You tell yourself it's politics. Sometimes it is. But mostly it isn't.

Here's what's actually happening.

Above the manager line, the job changes.

Below the manager line, you're paid for what you can DO. Above it, you're paid for what you can INFLUENCE. Director-level work is 20% technical, 80% translation, persuasion, budget defense, hiring, executive communication, and cross-functional politics.

When you interview for a Director role using your manager résumé and your manager language, you sound like a manager. And the person on the other side of the table — the CISO or the CIO — listens to you and thinks, "This person is technically strong. But I don't see the Director in them yet."

They're not wrong. They're just seeing what you're presenting.

The gap between "can do the job" and "can be seen doing the job" is the gap that's keeping you stuck. And it doesn't close on its own.

Part 4 — Why the Obvious Options Don't Work

Let me walk you through the paths most Security Managers try, and why most of them fail.

1. Another certification.

You already have CISSP. Maybe CISM. Maybe CCSP. You're thinking about CCISO, CGEIT, or TOGAF.

Here's the truth. Certs after CISSP have flat-lined as a promotion lever. I hire Directors and I don't look at their cert alphabet anymore. I look at their operational track record, their board exposure, and how they talk. An extra cert on your LinkedIn doesn't change how you talk in a Director interview.

Does that mean don't get them? No. If you want to get CCISO or CISSP-ISSMP, do it. Just know that the cert isn't what gets you the job. At best, it's table stakes.

2. An MBA or Master's in cybersecurity.

Two-year investment. $40K-$80K. Here's what actually happens when you finish: you come back to the same company, in the same role, and nobody promotes you because you completed a degree. The degree is positioning for your NEXT career, not your current company. And your next company still hires on positioning + network, not on diplomas.

If you love learning, great. If you're doing it for promotion, it's the slowest and most expensive option you can pick.

3. Job hopping.

This works — some of the time. You go from Manager at Company A to Manager at Company B for a 15-20% raise. Then Manager at Company C for another jump. After three hops you're making more money, but you're still a Manager.

The reason hopping doesn't fix the Director gap is that Company C is hiring for the same role Company A was hiring for. You're just getting paid more to do it. The positioning problem travels with you.

4. LinkedIn Learning, Coursera, YouTube.

All of it is free or almost free, and none of it makes you a Director. It's technical content. You don't have a technical deficit. You have a leadership presentation deficit.

5. Asking your current boss for a promotion.

Your boss isn't trained to promote you. Most CISOs and IT Directors I know didn't get promoted by their boss — they got promoted by building a case that was undeniable and sometimes by leaving and coming back at the next tier. Very few companies have a real promotion pipeline for senior security roles.

6. "I'll just network more."

Networking works if you know who you're networking with and what you're trying to extract. Most Security Managers network sideways — they know a lot of other Security Managers. That's the wrong net. You need a net of Directors, Heads, VPs, and CISOs who already hire for the role you want. That network doesn't get built by showing up to one more chapter meeting.

Part 5 — The Three Shifts (This Is the Mechanism)

OK, so what actually works.

In 28 years, I've identified three shifts that separate the Manager who stays stuck from the Manager who lands the Director role.

Every single person I've seen make the jump made these three shifts, whether they knew it or not.

Shift 1: POSITIONING

You stop sounding like an engineer.

When a Manager describes their job, they say: "I run the SOC. I manage 6 analysts. We handle 40K events a month. I implement controls, I respond to incidents, I report to the CISO."

That's accurate. It's also the exact wrong way to describe your job if you want a Director role.

When a Director describes their job, they say: "I own the organization's security posture across a $2B revenue business. My team reduced material risk exposure by 40% year-over-year, saved the company $1.2M in prevented fraud, and I represent security posture to the audit committee quarterly."

Same work. Completely different altitude.

Positioning is not lying. It's describing your work at the altitude of the person who's about to hire you.

Most Security Managers have never been trained to do this. We fix that in the first 4 weeks.

Shift 2: EXECUTIVE PRESENCE

Presence is a word people throw around without defining.

Here's my definition. Executive presence is the ability to walk into a room of CFOs, Chief Risk Officers, and board members, and explain security in the language THEY speak — risk, revenue, resilience, reputation — without dropping into acronyms or making them feel dumb.

Almost no Security Manager has this. Almost every Director has it.

The reason is brutal but simple. Nobody ever taught it. We get trained on MITRE ATT&CK. We don't get trained on board storytelling.

In the program, I give you the exact frameworks I've used for 28 years to translate security into C-suite language. You will practice these. You will be bad at them for the first two weeks. Then you will be good at them. This is the single highest-leverage skill you'll build.

Shift 3: DIRECTOR-LEVEL NETWORK

You need a peer network one level above your current role.

Not because networking magically gets you jobs. Because the Director role search is 80% warm intros and 20% cold applications. If you're applying cold to Director roles, you're competing against 400 other résumés. If you're being warm-intro'd by someone at the hiring company, you're one of 3 people they're seriously considering.

In the program, I introduce you to people in my network. I teach you how to activate your own network (yes, you already have one — you just haven't used it the right way). And I give you a 30-day plan for building a permanent peer network of Directors and VPs who can refer you for the rest of your career.

Those are the three shifts. Positioning. Executive Presence. Director Network.

Make them, you get promoted. Skip them, you stay stuck.

Part 11 — FAQ

I've had hundreds of these conversations over the years. Here are the honest answers to the questions almost everyone asks.

"I already have CISSP, CISM, CCSP. What more do you give me that I don't already have?"
None of those certs teach you positioning, executive presence, or network activation. They prove technical knowledge. The Director interview tests for business translation, leadership presence, and strategic thinking. Your certs are table stakes — they get you past the HR screen, not the CISO panel.
"I don't have time for 14 weeks."
Weekly session is 60-75 minutes. Between sessions, most students spend 3-5 hours on homework. Total time per week is about 5-6 hours. If you're working 50+ hours a week already and cannot find 5 more to invest in the next 10 years of your career, the program isn't your biggest problem.
"Can my employer pay for this?"
Often yes. I've had dozens of students expense this as professional development. Most employers have a professional development budget that's underused. I'll give you the 1-page justification document you can submit. Frame it as leadership development, not a cert prep course.
"Why not just apply for Director roles myself and see what happens?"
You can. Most Managers who try this get rejected 20+ times before they figure out what's wrong with their positioning. That's fine if you have 2-3 years to burn. The program compresses that entire feedback loop into 14 weeks by giving you the honest feedback most recruiters won't.
"What if the Director role I land pays less than $200K?"
Most don't. Some do. Even a $180K offer is $30K above your current salary, and the program still pays back in 4 months. Also — we don't aim for the lowest Director role in your city. We aim for the strongest one you're qualified for. That's part of the work.
"I don't want to be a CISO. I just want to be a Director. Is this right for me?"
Yes. This program targets Director-level roles specifically. If you later decide you want to go CISO, the same framework applies at the next tier. But the goal here is Director.
"I'm in a specialized niche — cloud security / GRC / offensive security. Does this still work?"
Yes. I've placed students into Director roles across cloud security, GRC, AppSec, SOC, offensive security, and red team leadership. The positioning changes by niche. The mechanism doesn't.

"What if I don't get a Director offer in 14 weeks?"
Most students don't have an offer in hand in exactly 14 weeks. The program builds the foundation. Most offers land between month 4 and month 9 after we finish. You'll know after Week 4 whether you're on track. If you do the work, the outcome isn't a question of if — it's a question of when.

I don't offer a money-back guarantee because I've seen guarantees attract people who are looking for an excuse not to do the work.
